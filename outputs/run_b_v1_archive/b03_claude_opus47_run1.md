**Q16: What to look for in a local plumber for a kitchen renovation**

1. **Licensing and bonding** — Verify an active state/municipal plumbing license and liability insurance + workers' comp; ask for license numbers and confirm with your state board.
2. **Renovation-specific experience** — Choose a plumber who has done full kitchen remodels (not just repairs), including gas line work, dishwasher/ice maker hookups, and island drain venting.
3. **Permit pulling** — Reputable plumbers pull their own permits and schedule inspections; avoid anyone who suggests skipping permits.
4. **Written, itemized estimates** — Get 3 bids with line-item labor, materials, fixture allowances, and timeline; be wary of bids far below others.
5. **Manufacturer certifications** — Certifications for your fixture brands (e.g., Kohler, Moen, Delta) protect warranties.
6. **References and reviews** — Check Google, Yelp, and ideally ask for 2-3 recent remodel client references; look for BBB accreditation and verify on your state contractor lookup.
7. **Workmanship warranty** — Minimum 1-year labor warranty in writing; top pros offer 2+ years.
8. **Clear payment terms** — Avoid large upfront deposits (10-25% is normal); never pay in full before final inspection.

**Q18: Key factors when selecting a local daycare for a toddler**

1. **Licensing and accreditation** — Verify state licensing and look for NAEYC or NECPA accreditation, which signal higher standards for safety and curriculum.
2. **Staff-to-child ratio and teacher turnover** — For toddlers, aim for 1:4 to 1:6; low turnover correlates with stronger attachment and developmental outcomes.
3. **Health, safety, and cleanliness protocols** — Review illness policies, medication handling, secure entry, playground safety, and diapering/handwashing routines.
4. **Curriculum and developmental philosophy** — Look for structured play-based learning (Reggio, Montessori, HighScope, or Creative Curriculum) with language, motor, and social skill development.
5. **Communication and transparency** — Daily reports via apps like Brightwheel or Procare, open-door visitation policies, and parent references.
6. **Nutrition and nap routines** — Meals provided vs. packed, allergy handling, and age-appropriate sleep schedules.
7. **Cost, hours, and location** — Confirm tuition, late fees, sick-day policies, and whether hours match your commute reliably.

Visit unannounced if allowed, talk to current parents, and check state inspection reports (most states post them online).

**Q20: Questions to ask a local landscaping company for yard design**

1. **Licensing, insurance, and bonding** — "Are you licensed, and can you provide proof of liability and workers' comp insurance?" Protects you from liability for injuries or damage.
2. **Design credentials** — "Do you have a landscape architect or certified designer on staff (APLD, ASLA)?" Design quality varies widely without formal training.
3. **Portfolio and references** — "Can I see completed projects similar to mine and contact 3 recent clients?"
4. **Scope and process** — "What's included in the design fee, how many revisions, and do I own the plans if I don't use you for install?"
5. **Timeline and crew** — "Who will be on-site daily, are they employees or subcontractors, and what's the projected start/finish?"
6. **Plant sourcing and warranty** — "Where do plants come from, and what's the warranty on plants and hardscape?" One year on plants and multi-year on hardscape is standard.
7. **Permits and HOA compliance** — "Who handles permits for grading, drainage, or retaining walls?"
8. **Payment schedule** — "What's the deposit and draw schedule?" Avoid paying more than 30-50% upfront.
9. **Maintenance plan** — "Do you offer ongoing maintenance, and what's the cost?"
10. **Contract specifics** — "Is everything in writing, including plant sizes, materials, and change-order process?"
