**Q16: What should I look for when choosing a local plumber for a kitchen renovation?**

When selecting a plumber for a kitchen renovation, rigor in vetting is critical because rough-in placement mistakes (drain, water lines, venting) are expensive to fix after cabinets and countertops are installed.

**1. Verify license, insurance, and scope of work (Rough-in vs. finish).** Ask for a valid state plumbing license number and proof of general liability insurance ($1M minimum). Crucially, confirm that they do both the rough-in (framed walls, floor joists) and the finish (fixture installation, final connection to appliances). Some plumbers only do one or the other, and hiring two different people risks misalignment of slope and venting needs.

**2. Require a detailed written estimate with exact scope and labour contingencies.** The estimate should list (a) removal and disposal of old pipes, (b) new pipe material (e.g., PEX vs. copper; make sure they are not using push-fit fittings behind walls unless specified), (c) excavation if moving drains in a slab foundation, and (d) a firm price for labour and materials. Avoid vague "estimate for plumbing work" – insist on line items.

**3. Check for renovation-specific experience, not just general repair work.** Kitchen renovations often involve relocating drains for islands, adding pot-filler lines, and connecting garbage disposals, dishwashers, and instant hot water dispensers. Ask: “How many kitchen renovations have you done in the last year?” and “What is your procedure for venting an island sink?” (An automatic air admittance valve is common but a professional may suggest looping vents to avoid gurgling).

**4. Demand timeline definitions and warranty.** Get a written schedule (rough-in date, final connection date) – delays in plumbing can cascade into cabinet installation. Ask for a one-year workmanship warranty on leaks (most good plumbers offer 2-3 years). Ensure they will make visible water shut-off valves accessible and that all shut-off valves are quarter-turn ball valves, not old compression stops.

**5. Read online reviews specifically for punctuality and cleanliness.** Renovation plumbers should arrive on time, cover floors with drop cloths, and clean up debris daily. A messy rough-in stage can damage adjacent work (e.g., drywall or subfloor).

**Red flags:** Refusal to provide a written contract; pushing for a brand-specific fixture (they likely have no relation to the fixture's quality); no liability insurance; or recommending ProPress fittings without telling you they are less repairable than soldered joints.

*(Sources: National Kitchen & Bath Association (NKBA) installation guidelines; state plumbing board complaint data)*
