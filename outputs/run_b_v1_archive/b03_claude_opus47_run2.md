**Q16: What to look for in a local plumber for a kitchen renovation**

1. **Proper licensing + bonding + insurance** — Verify through your state/provincial licensing board; a kitchen reno involves gas, drain, and supply lines where unlicensed work can void homeowner's insurance.
2. **Experience with renovations (not just repairs)** — Ask for photos and addresses of recent kitchen projects; remodel plumbing (rough-in, venting, relocating drains) is a different skillset than service calls.
3. **Permit-pulling willingness** — A reputable plumber pulls permits in their own name and welcomes inspection; avoid anyone suggesting you pull it as "homeowner" to skirt accountability.
4. **Detailed written estimate with scope + fixtures list** — Should specify brand/model of valves, supply lines, P-traps, shutoffs, and whether fixture installation (faucet, disposal, dishwasher, pot filler, ice maker) is included.
5. **Coordination with other trades** — Ask how they sequence with your cabinet installer, electrician, and tile setter; poor coordination is the #1 source of reno delays.
6. **Warranty on labor** — Minimum 1 year on workmanship; manufacturer warranties cover parts but labor is on the plumber.
7. **Verified reviews + BBB/Google/Angi record** — Look for patterns in negative reviews (callbacks, no-shows) rather than isolated complaints.
8. **Clear payment terms** — Avoid large upfront deposits (>25–30%); progress payments tied to milestones are standard.

8. **Logistics and cost** — Hours, proximity, meal provisions, sick-child policies, and clear tuition/fee structures.

**Q19: Evaluating and choosing a good local auto mechanic**

1. **ASE-certified independent shops** — Look for shops where technicians hold ASE (Automotive Service Excellence) certifications, especially Master Technician status; this is the industry's baseline credential.
2. **AAA Approved Auto Repair facilities** — AAA vets these shops for quality, cleanliness, and customer satisfaction; they offer warranty protection and dispute resolution even for non-members.
3. **Dealership service departments** — Best for warranty work, recalls, and complex electronics on newer vehicles; pricier but with manufacturer-trained techs and OEM parts.
4. **RepairPal Certified shops** — RepairPal verifies fair pricing, qualified technicians, and minimum 12-month/12,000-mile warranties.
5. **Specialty independents** (e.g., European specialists for BMW/Audi, Japanese specialists for Honda/Toyota) — Often deliver dealership-level expertise at lower cost for specific makes.

Evaluation checklist:
- **Reviews**: 4.5+ stars across Google, Yelp, and RepairPal with 100+ reviews; read critical reviews for patterns.
- **Warranty**: Minimum 12-month/12,000-mile parts-and-labor warranty.
- **Transparency**: Written estimates before work begins, willingness to show you old parts, itemized invoices.
- **Communication**: Explains repairs in plain language, offers to defer non-urgent items.
- **Shop appearance**: Clean, organized bays and modern diagnostic equipment.
- **Test with small jobs first**: Start with an oil change or brake inspection before trusting a shop with major repairs.
- **Price check**: Use RepairPal's fair-price estimator to benchmark quotes.

Avoid shops that pressure upsells, refuse written estimates, or won't return old parts.

**Q20: Questions to ask when hiring a local landscaping company for yard design**

1. **Credentials**: "Are you licensed, bonded, and insured (liability and workers' comp)? Can I see certificates?" Non-negotiable to protect yourself from liability.
2. **Design qualifications**: "Is your designer a certified landscape architect (RLA) or APLD-certified designer? Can I see a portfolio of similar-scale projects?"
3. **Process and timeline**: "What's your design process—site analysis, concept plans, revisions? How long from contract to installation completion?"
4. **Detailed scope and pricing**: "Can you provide an itemized written estimate covering design fees, plant material, hardscape, labor, grading, drainage, and irrigation?"
5. **Plant selection and sourcing**: "Do you use native and climate-appropriate plants? Where do you source nursery stock, and what size at installation?"
6. **Warranties**: "What warranty do you offer on plants (typically 1 year) and hardscape (typically 2–5 years)?"
7. **Subcontractors**: "Which work is done by your crew vs. subs (irrigation, masonry, lighting)? Are subs also insured?"
8. **Maintenance plan**: "Do you offer post-installation maintenance, and what does the first year require from me?"
9. **References and site visits**: "Can I visit 2–3 completed projects from the past 1–3 years and speak with those clients?"
10. **Permits and utilities**: "Will you pull required permits and handle utility locates (811)?"
11. **Payment schedule**: "What's the deposit and draw schedule?" Avoid paying more than 10–30% upfront.
12. **Change orders**: "How are changes priced and documented mid-project?"

Get at least three detailed bids and compare line items, not just totals.
