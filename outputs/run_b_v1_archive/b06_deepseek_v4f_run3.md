**Q16: What to look for when choosing a local plumber for a kitchen renovation**
1. **Licensed, Insured, and Bonded** — Non-negotiable: verify the plumber holds a valid state license (often Master Plumber designation), carries general liability insurance (minimum $1M), and workers’ compensation coverage. This protects you from damages to your renovation and covers injury liabilities on site. Request their license number and verify it online.
2. **Specialization in Renovation Work vs. Service Calls** — A plumber who focuses on new construction or remodels will be familiar with open-wall installation, rough-in for new appliances (dishwasher, disposal, instant hot water faucet), and code-compliance for expanding existing drain lines. Service plumbers excel at unclogging but may lack framing and drywall coordination skills.
3. **Transparent, Itemized Bidding** — Avoid plumbers who give a simple “$5,000 total” quote. Instead, look for a scope breakdown: materials markup (should be 10-15% over wholesale), fixture installation labor per unit, and any potential costs for unexpected wall rot or outdated galvanized pipe. Ask for a “time and materials not-to-exceed” clause to cap surprises.
4. **Local Reputation and Recent Renovation Portfolio** — Ask for 2-3 references from kitchen-specific projects completed in the last year. Visit those jobs (if permitted) to check finish work: are supply lines tidily run? Is the trap accessible? Are shut-off valves easy to reach? A strong Yelp or Nextdoor rating from local homeowners is helpful, but a direct inspection of final work is better.
5. **Communication and Schedule Guarantee** — Renovations are emotional; choose a plumber who returns calls within 24 hours, uses a project management app to track milestones, and can clearly explain how they’ll sequence work with your electrician and countertop installers. Delays must be communicated in writing to protect your renovation timeline.
*Note: These criteria are based on best practices from the National Kitchen and Bath Association (NKBA) and common homeowner litigation patterns.*
