**Q16: What to look for in a local plumber for a kitchen renovation**

1. **Licensed and bonded in your state/municipality** — Verify through your state licensing board; a renovation involves permitted work, and unlicensed labor can void insurance and home sale disclosures.
2. **Specific kitchen renovation experience** — Ask for recent project photos and references; kitchen work involves gas lines, dishwasher/disposal/ice maker hookups, and venting that differ from basic repair plumbing.
3. **Pulls permits and coordinates inspections** — A reputable plumber handles permitting directly rather than asking you to DIY it; this protects resale value.
4. **Detailed written estimate with scope and materials** — Should specify fixture brands, pipe materials (PEX vs. copper), and labor separately; beware lump-sum quotes.
5. **Insurance: general liability AND workers' comp** — Request current certificates; without workers' comp, an on-site injury can become your liability.
6. **Strong local reviews across multiple platforms** — Cross-check Google, Yelp, Angi, and the BBB; look for patterns in reviews mentioning punctuality, cleanup, and warranty follow-through.
7. **Warranty on labor (minimum 1 year)** — Reputable shops back their work; also confirm they honor manufacturer warranties on fixtures you supply.
8. **Clear communication and coordination with your GC/cabinet installer** — Timing of rough-in and finish plumbing is critical in a renovation; ask how they coordinate schedules.

**Q18: Most important factors when selecting a local daycare for a toddler**

1. **Licensing and accreditation** — Verify state licensure and look for NAEYC accreditation, which signals higher standards in curriculum, safety, and staff qualifications.
2. **Staff-to-child ratio and turnover** — For toddlers, aim for 1:4 to 1:6; ask how long lead teachers have been there, as low turnover correlates strongly with quality.
3. **Safety and cleanliness protocols** — Secure entry, illness policies, diapering/handwashing routines, and emergency drills should be clearly documented.
4. **Curriculum and developmental approach** — Whether Montessori, Reggio, play-based, or HighScope, the program should be age-appropriate with daily structure and outdoor time.
5. **Communication and transparency** — Apps like Brightwheel or Procare for daily updates, open-door visiting policies, and regular parent-teacher conferences.
6. **Inspection history** — Pull state licensing reports; repeated violations are a red flag.
7. **Cost, hours, and logistics** — Confirm pricing, backup care, sick-day policies, and proximity to home/work.

Visit at least three centers unannounced during mid-morning, observe teacher-child interactions (are they warm and at eye level?), and talk to current parents at pickup. Trust your instincts during the tour.

**Q20: Questions to ask when hiring a local landscaping company for yard design**

1. **Credentials and insurance** — "Are you licensed, bonded, and insured for liability and worker's comp? Can I see certificates?" APLD (Association of Professional Landscape Designers) or state landscape architect licensure indicates design credibility.
2. **Portfolio and references** — "Can I see 3–5 completed projects similar in scope and budget to mine, and speak to those clients?"
3. **Design process and deliverables** — "Do you provide scaled drawings, 3D renderings, and a plant schedule? Is design fee separate from installation?"
4. **Plant selection philosophy** — "Do you prioritize native and climate-appropriate plants? How do you plan for mature size and seasonal interest?"
5. **Timeline and project management** — "Who is my point of contact, and how are change orders handled?"
6. **Warranty** — "What's your warranty on plants (typically 1 year) and hardscape (typically 2–5 years)?"
7. **Subcontractors** — "Is irrigation, lighting, or masonry done in-house or subbed out, and who warranties that work?"
8. **Maintenance plan** — "Do you offer post-installation maintenance, and what's required in year one?"
9. **Payment schedule** — "What's the deposit and draw schedule?" Avoid anyone demanding more than 30–50% upfront.
10. **Permits** — "Will you pull required permits for grading, drainage, or structures?"

Get three written bids with itemized materials and labor, and be cautious of quotes dramatically below others—cheap plants and poor soil prep lead to replacement costs within two years.
