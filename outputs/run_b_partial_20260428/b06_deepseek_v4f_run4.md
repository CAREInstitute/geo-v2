**Q16: What should I look for when choosing a local plumber for a kitchen renovation?**

1. **Verify Licensing and Insurance (General & Workers’ Comp)** — This is non-negotiable. Ask for the plumber’s state license number (e.g., Journeyman or Master) and confirm it online. Also request a certificate of general liability insurance ($1M+ minimum) and workers’ compensation insurance. This protects you if they damage your house or get injured on the job.
2. **Request and Check References for Kitchen Renovation Projects** — Not just any plumbing job. Ask for specific references from the last 12 months on remodels where the plumber roughed in new supply lines, moved drains for a new sink/ dishwasher location, and connected a garbage disposal or pot filler. Contact those homeowners to ask about on-time completion, cleanliness, and change-order pricing.
3. **Get a Detailed Written Quote, Not an Estimate** — A proper quote should itemize materials (e.g., brand of faucet, specific PEX or copper piping, shutoff valves) and labor for each phase: rough-in, water line connection, fixture installation, final testing. Avoid hourly-only plumbers for kitchen remodels; demand a fixed-price quote for the scope of work. Ask about permitting fees (many jurisdictions require plumbing permits).
4. **Confirm Their Experience with Modern Kitchen Fixtures** — Ensure they are comfortable installing point-of-use water heaters (under-sink), touchless faucets, pot fillers, and multi-function sprayers. Some older plumbers may try to dissuade you from complex fixtures they don't understand. Ask about their familiarity with PEX-A expansion systems vs. PEX-B crimp, as many modern remodels use PEX-A for durability.
5. **Ask About Their "Clean & Protect" Policy** — Kitchen remodels generate dust and debris. Ask how they will protect your cabinetry, floors, and countertops during work (drop cloths, shoe covers, negative air machines). Also ask about their policy for cleaning up after the job (e.g., running water to flush debris, vacuuming under sinks, removing all old material).

*Sources: Angi (formerly Angie’s List) contractor vetting guides, National Kitchen and Bath Association (NKBA) best practices, local state licensing board requirements (varies by state).*
